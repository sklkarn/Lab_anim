Objective

To map equipotential lines and electric field lines in the region between a positive charge (+q) and a negative charge (−q) using a conducting paper analog, and to understand the relationship between electric potential and electric field direction.

Equipotential Lines

Lines connecting points of equal electric potential. No work is done moving a charge along an equipotential surface.

Electric Field Lines

Lines showing the direction of the electric force on a positive test charge. They always point from high to low potential.

Perpendicularity Rule

Electric field lines are always perpendicular (90°) to equipotential lines at every point in space.

Field Strength

The magnitude of the electric field between two equipotentials:

E = −ΔV / Δd

Closer equipotential lines = stronger field.

How the Conducting Paper Works

Conducting (resistive) paper is a uniform resistor. When a DC voltage is applied across two electrodes painted on its surface, a steady current flows through the paper. Because the sheet is homogeneous, the potential distribution within it is mathematically identical to a 2D electrostatic field. A voltmeter probe scanned across the paper reads the local electric potential — allowing you to trace equipotential lines without disturbing the field.

Lab Procedure

Follow each step carefully. Read all warnings before starting.

⚠ Safety: This experiment uses low DC voltages (6–9 V) which are safe. Never touch live bare connections simultaneously. Keep the conducting paper dry. Do not short-circuit the battery.
Part A — Preparing the Conducting Paper
01
Set up the surface

Place the conducting paper (≈30×30 cm) on a flat, non-conductive surface such as a wooden board or thick cardboard.

Setup
02
Paint the electrodes

Using silver conductive paint, draw two filled circles (~1 cm diameter) separated by 12–15 cm near the center of the paper. Label the left dot +q and the right dot −q. Allow paint to dry 5–10 minutes.

Setup
03
Insert contact pins

Press a metal pin or copper tack into the center of each painted electrode to ensure good electrical contact.

Setup
04
Attach graph paper overlay

Tape a graph paper sheet on top of the conducting paper. You will mark probe positions on this overlay.

Setup
Part B — Wiring the Circuit
05
Connect the battery

Connect the positive (+) terminal of the battery to the +q electrode. Connect the negative (−) terminal to the −q electrode using alligator clip wires.

Wiring
06
Set voltmeter reference

Connect the COM (black) probe of the voltmeter to the −q electrode. This sets −q as ground (0 V reference).

Wiring
Tip: Verify your setup — touch the red probe to +q (should read ≈battery voltage) and to −q (should read ≈0 V).
Part C — Mapping Equipotential Lines
07
Choose target voltages

Select 5–6 voltage levels to map. For a 6 V battery: 1.0 V, 2.0 V, 3.0 V, 4.0 V, 5.0 V.

Measure
08
Scan and mark points

Gently touch the red probe to the paper. Move it until the voltmeter reads your target voltage. Mark the position on the graph paper overlay. Record x-y coordinates and voltage in the Data Table.

Measure
09
Collect 8–10 points per voltage

Spread points above, between, and below the electrodes. Then connect the dots for each voltage level with a smooth curve. Label each curve with its voltage.

Measure
Part D — Drawing Electric Field Lines
10
Draw perpendicular lines

Using a ruler and compass, draw lines that cross each equipotential at exactly 90°. Start from +q and end at −q.

Draw
11
Add arrows and analyze spacing

Draw at least 8 field lines. Add arrowheads pointing from +q toward −q. Observe where lines are closely spaced (strong field) vs. widely spaced (weak field).

Draw

Analysis Questions

Answer in complete sentences. Reference your data and field map where applicable. — Dr. S Karna

Q1 — Equipotential Shape

Describe the general shape of the equipotential lines near the +q electrode and the −q electrode. Are they circular near the charges? How do they change in the region between the two charges? Explain why.

Q2 — Field Strength

Where on your map is the electric field the strongest? How can you tell from the spacing of the equipotential lines?

Q3 — Perpendicularity Rule

At what angle do the electric field lines cross the equipotential lines? Is this consistent across the entire map? What physical law does this illustrate?

Q4 — Force on Test Charge

A positive test charge is placed midway between +q and −q on the horizontal axis. Using your map, predict the direction of the force on this charge. Justify your answer using your field line diagram.

Q5 — Effect of Voltage

If you doubled the battery voltage, how would the equipotential line positions change? Would the electric field line pattern (shape) change? Explain your reasoning.

Q6 — Numerical Calculation

Using your data table, calculate the approximate electric field strength (E = ΔV / Δd) between two adjacent equipotential lines in the region directly between the two charges. Show all work with units.

Conclusion

In 3–5 sentences, summarize what you learned about electric field lines and equipotential lines. Discuss at least two potential sources of experimental error and their likely impact on your results.
