Hadean 4,600 – 4,000 Ma ▼

Formation of Earth, Moon, oceans, and earliest crust. Intense meteorite bombardment. No preserved rocks.

Pre-Nectarian

4,600 – 4,100 Ma

▼

Earth forms from solar nebula. Magma ocean solidifies. Earliest Moon-forming impact (~4,510 Ma).

Formation

4,600 – 4,510 Ma

▼

Accretion of Earth from protoplanetary disk. Differentiation into core, mantle, crust.

Earth accretesCore forms
Giant Impact ~4,510 Ma Theia collides with proto-Earth; debris forms the Moon. Earth tilts 23.5° on axis.
Magma Ocean 4,510–4,400 Ma Global magma ocean cools over millions of years. Heavy bombardment by planetesimals.

4,100 – 3,800 Ma

▼

Intense asteroid bombardment. Possible delivery of water by carbonaceous chondrites. Earliest zircons (~4,404 Ma).

Heavy impactsOceans form
Jack Hills Zircons 4,404 Ma Oldest known minerals on Earth. Evidence for liquid water and proto-continental crust.
First Oceans ~4,300 Ma Liquid water stabilizes on surface. Outgassing and cometary delivery. Anoxic atmosphere of N₂, CO₂, H₂O.
Archean 4,000 – 2,500 Ma ▼

First stable cratons. Earliest life (stromatolites). Anoxic oceans. No ozone layer.

Eoarchean

4,000 – 3,600 Ma

▼

Formation of first stable continental crust (Acasta Gneiss, ~4,031 Ma). Isua greenstone belt (Greenland) — earliest metasedimentary rocks.

First Crust

Paleoarchean

Neoarchean

2,800 – 2,500 Ma

▼

Peak continental crust formation. First supercontinent Vaalbara. Oxygenic photosynthesis begins. Banded iron formations.

Oxygenic Photosynthesis

2,700 – 2,500 Ma

▼

Cyanobacteria evolve. Begin releasing O₂ into ocean. Banded iron formations record early oxygen. Supercontinent Kenorland forms.

CyanobacteriaBanded iron formationsO₂ rises

Kenorland ~2,700 Ma First proposed supercontinent assembles. Tectonic recycling accelerates. Greenstone belts form.
Proterozoic 2,500 – 541 Ma ▼

Great Oxidation Event. Snowball Earth glaciations. Eukaryotes and multicellular life emerge. Rodinia & Pangaea precursors.

Paleoproterozoic

2,500 – 1,600 Ma

▼

Great Oxidation Event (~2,400 Ma) transforms atmosphere. Huronian glaciation. First eukaryotes (~2,100 Ma). Columbia supercontinent.

Siderian / GOE

2,500 – 2,050 Ma

▼

Great Oxidation Event: atmospheric O₂ rises from ~0% to ~1–2%. Huronian Glaciation. Methane greenhouse collapse. "Oxygen catastrophe" for anaerobes.

Great Oxidation EventAnaerobe die-offHuronian Glaciation
GOE Onset ~2,400 Ma Atmospheric oxygen rises sharply. Methane destroyed, triggering global cooling. Redbeds appear in rock record.
Huronian Ice Age 2,400–2,100 Ma Possibly earliest near-global glaciation. Three distinct glacial episodes recorded in Canadian Shield.

Rhyacian – Statherian

▼

First eukaryotes (Grypania spiralis ~2,100 Ma). Columbia/Nuna supercontinent assembles. Endosymbiosis hypothesis origins.

First eukaryotesColumbia supercontinent
Grypania ~2,100 Ma Coiled eukaryotic alga — first evidence of nucleus-bearing cells. Marks dawn of complexity.
Columbia Assembly ~1,800 Ma Supercontinent Columbia (Nuna) fully assembled. Interior continental environment dominates.

Mesoproterozoic

1,600 – 1,000 Ma

▼

"The Boring Billion" — relative biological and climatic stability. Sexual reproduction evolves. Rodinia assembles.

Calymmian – Ectasian

▼

Columbia breaks apart. Platform covers spread. Sexual reproduction first documented (~1,200 Ma red algae). Multicellular algae diversify.

Sexual reproductionColumbia rifts

Stenian / Rodinia

1,200 – 1,000 Ma

▼

Grenvillian orogeny. Rodinia supercontinent assembles (~1,100 Ma). Greenschist metamorphism widespread.

Rodinia formsGrenvillian orogeny
Rodinia Assembly ~1,100–1,000 Ma All major cratons merge into Rodinia. Located near equator. Grenville mountain belt rivaling modern Himalayas.

Neoproterozoic

1,000 – 541 Ma

▼

Snowball Earth events. Ediacaran biota — first animals. Rodinia breaks up. Cambrian explosion precursors.

Cryogenian

720 – 635 Ma

▼

Sturtian (~720 Ma) and Marinoan (~635 Ma) glaciations — ice sheets reach equator (Snowball Earth). Rodinia rifts apart.

Snowball Earth ×2Rodinia breaks up
Sturtian Glaciation 717–660 Ma First Snowball Earth event. Global average temperature ~−50°C. Equatorial ice sheets kilometres thick.
Marinoan Glaciation 650–635 Ma Second global glaciation. Ends abruptly with extreme greenhouse CO₂ buildup. Cap carbonates deposited worldwide.

Ediacaran

635 – 541 Ma

▼

Ediacaran biota — first macroscopic multicellular animals (Dickinsonia, Charnia). Nervous system precursors. Avalon Explosion.

First animalsEdiacaran biotaEdiacaran mass extinction
Avalon Explosion ~575 Ma Sudden appearance of frond-like Ediacaran organisms. Rangeomorpha dominate. Soft-bodied, no skeletons.
Dickinsonia ~558 Ma Oval quilted organism up to 1.4 m. Confirmed animal by cholesterol biomarkers. Moves and feeds on microbial mats.
Ediacaran Extinction ~541 Ma All Ediacaran organisms disappear. Replaced by Cambrian fauna. Possibly outcompeted by motile, predatory animals.
Paleozoic 541 – 252 Ma ▼

Cambrian Explosion to Great Dying. Fish, amphibians, reptiles, insects. Gondwana & Pangaea. Five major mass extinctions.

Cambrian

541 – 485 Ma

▼

Cambrian Explosion (~539 Ma): most animal phyla appear. Trilobites dominate. Burgess Shale fauna. Simple ecosystems diversify rapidly.

Terreneuvian

541 – 521 Ma

▼

Explosion of skeletal fauna. Small shelly fossils. Earliest trilobites (~521 Ma). Cambrian substrate revolution.

Cambrian ExplosionFirst trilobites
Fortunian 541–529 Ma Small shelly fossils (SSFs) appear. Cloudina and Namacalathus — first skeletal organisms. Trace fossils indicate burrowing.
Age 2 529–521 Ma Archaeocyatha reefs. Earliest trilobites (Fallotaspis). Chengjiang Biota — remarkable soft-tissue preservation.

Furongian

497 – 485 Ma

▼

Late Cambrian extinction events (SPICE event). Archaeocyatha decline. Echinoderms and brachiopods diversify.

SPICE extinctionEchinoderms diversify
SPICE Event ~499 Ma Steptoean Positive Carbon Isotope Excursion — oceanic anoxia causes major trilobite turnover. ~40% genera lost.

Ordovician

485 – 444 Ma

▼

GOBE: Great Ordovician Biodiversification. Marine invertebrates dominate. First land plants (mosses). Ends with 1st mass extinction.

Great Biodiversification

485 – 460 Ma

▼

Marine diversity triples. Graptolites, crinoids, brachiopods flourish. Jawless fish (ostracoderms). First terrestrial organisms (liverworts ~470 Ma).

GOBEFirst land plantsFirst fish
Floian Stage 477–470 Ma Peak Ordovician diversity. Graptolite zones used for global correlation. Nautiloid cephalopods become apex predators.
First Land Plants ~470 Ma Cryptospore pollen indicates first non-vascular land plants (liverwort-like). Colonization of terrestrial environments begins.

End-Ordovician Extinction

445 – 444 Ma

▼

2nd largest extinction. ~85% marine species lost in 2 pulses. Gondwana glaciation drops sea levels. Graptolites decimated.

Mass extinction #1Gondwana glaciationSea level drop
Pulse 1 (Hirnantian) 445.2 Ma Glaciation causes sea regression. Cold-water brachiopod fauna (Hirnantia) briefly dominates before collapse.
Pulse 2 (Rhuddanian) 443.8 Ma Ice melts rapidly, anoxic waters flood shelves. ~57% of genera extinct. Graptolites reduced to a few lineages.

Silurian

444 – 419 Ma

▼

Recovery and diversification. Jawed fish appear. First vascular land plants. Coral reefs re-establish. Stable warm climate.

Llandovery – Wenlock

444 – 427 Ma

▼

Marine recovery. First jawed fish (acanthodians). Eurypterids (sea scorpions) up to 2.5 m. Ozone layer thickens, enabling land colonization.

Jawed fish evolveVascular plantsLand arthropods
Cooksonia ~430 Ma Earliest vascular plant — simple dichotomous stems with terminal sporangia. No leaves or roots yet.
First Land Arthropods ~428 Ma Pneumodesmus (millipede) — oldest known air-breathing land animal. Begins arthropod terrestrial radiation.

Devonian

419 – 359 Ma

▼

"Age of Fishes." First forests, seeds, amphibians. Lobe-finned fish crawl ashore. Late Devonian extinction kills ~75% species.

Early Devonian

419 – 393 Ma

▼

Diversification of fish. Placoderms and sharks evolve. First insects (~385 Ma). Rhyniophytes give way to true forests.

Age of FishesFirst insectsFirst forests
Dunkleosteus ~380 Ma Giant armored placoderm fish — 6 m long with self-sharpening bone blades. Apex predator of Devonian seas.
Archaeopteris Forests ~385 Ma First true forests with deep roots. Dramatically increase weathering, drawing down CO₂ and causing ocean anoxia.

Tiktaalik & Tetrapods

375 – 360 Ma

▼

Tiktaalik roseae (~375 Ma) — transitional fish-tetrapod. Acanthostega, Ichthyostega walk on land. First amniote eggs.

First tetrapodsConquest of land
Tiktaalik 375 Ma "Fishapod" with wrist bones, neck, and ribs. Could prop itself on fins. Transitional between fish and tetrapods.
Kelvingrove Tetrapods ~365 Ma Acanthostega and Ichthyostega — first fully-limbed vertebrates. 8 digits on each limb. Semi-aquatic lifestyle.

Late Devonian Extinction

375 – 359 Ma

▼

Kellwasser and Hangenberg events kill ~75% species. Reefs collapse globally. Placoderms and many fish families go extinct.

Mass extinction #2Ocean anoxiaReef collapse
Kellwasser Event 372 Ma Two pulses of oceanic anoxia. Global cooling possibly triggered by forest expansion removing CO₂. Reef ecosystems obliterated.
Hangenberg Event 359 Ma Final pulse. Glaciation causes sea-level fall. Placoderms entirely extinct. Marks Devonian–Carboniferous boundary.

Carboniferous

359 – 299 Ma

▼

Coal swamp forests. High oxygen (35%). Giant insects. First reptiles (amniotes). Gondwana ice age. Pangaea begins forming.

Mississippian

359 – 323 Ma

▼

Extensive shallow seas over North America. Crinoid seas. Giant amphibians. O₂ levels rise to ~30%. Crinoid limestone deposits.

Giant amphibiansO₂ rises to 30%
Crinoid Seas 345–323 Ma Echinoderm paradise — crinoid meadows cover shallow seas. Their remains form thick limestone beds exploited today.

Pennsylvanian

323 – 299 Ma

▼

Coal swamp forests (Lepidodendron, Sigillaria). Giant Meganeura dragonflies (75 cm). First reptiles (~315 Ma). Rainforest collapse ~307 Ma.

First reptilesGiant insectsCoal forestsRainforest collapse
Hylonomus ~315 Ma Oldest known reptile. Small lizard-like amniote. Lays eggs on land — key evolutionary step freeing vertebrates from water.
Carboniferous Rainforest Collapse ~307 Ma Climate shift fragments continuous forests into isolated patches. Drives speciation among amphibians and reptiles.
Meganeura 300 Ma Griffinfly with 70 cm wingspan — largest flying insect ever. Enabled by hyperoxic atmosphere (~35% O₂).

Permian

299 – 252 Ma

▼

Pangaea fully assembled. Therapsids (mammal ancestors) dominate. Ends with THE GREAT DYING — worst extinction in Earth history (~96% species).

Early–Middle Permian

299 – 260 Ma

▼

Synapsids diversify. Dimetrodon (pelycosaur). Therapsids (Gorgonopsians) become apex predators. Conifers spread across dry Pangaea interior.

Therapsids dominantPangaea complete
Dimetrodon ~295 Ma Iconic sail-backed synapsid — closer relative of mammals than reptiles. Apex predator of Early Permian.
Gorgonopsians ~270 Ma Saber-toothed therapsids up to 3 m. Fast, active hunters. Dominated terrestrial ecosystems until the Great Dying.

THE GREAT DYING

~252 Ma

▼

End-Permian extinction: ~96% marine species, ~70% terrestrial vertebrates gone. Siberian Traps eruption releases CO₂/SO₂. Ocean acidification. Took 5–10 million years to recover.

WORST EXTINCTION EVERSiberian TrapsOcean acidification+10°C warming
Siberian Traps 252.2 Ma Largest known continental flood basalt eruption (~3M km²). Releases CO₂, SO₂, HCl. Triggers lethal warming and acid rain.
Marine Collapse 252.1 Ma Trilobites completely extinct. Rugose and tabulate corals gone. ~96% species lost. Took 50,000 years for full collapse.
Lystrosaurus Monopoly 251 Ma Pig-sized therapsid Lystrosaurus comprises ~95% of land vertebrates post-extinction — extreme ecological simplification.
Mesozoic 252 – 66 Ma ▼

Age of Dinosaurs. Mammals & birds evolve. Flowering plants appear. Pangaea rifts into modern continents. K-Pg asteroid impact.

Triassic

252 – 201 Ma

▼

Recovery from Great Dying. First dinosaurs (~230 Ma). First mammals (~225 Ma). Pangaea begins rifting. Ends with end-Triassic extinction.

Early–Middle Triassic

252 – 237 Ma

▼

Slow recovery. Archosaurs diverge into dinosaur and crocodilian lineages. First pterosaurs (~230 Ma). Eosaurians radiate.

Archosaur radiationFirst pterosaurs
First Dinosaurs ~231 Ma Eoraptor and Herrerasaurus (Argentina) — earliest dinosaurs. Small, bipedal carnivores.
First Mammals ~225 Ma Adelobasileus and Morganucodon — earliest mammaliaforms. Small, nocturnal insectivores with differentiated teeth.

End-Triassic Extinction

~201 Ma

▼

CAMP volcanism (Central Atlantic Magmatic Province). ~76% species lost. Eliminates crurotarsans, allows dinosaur dominance.

Mass extinction #4CAMP volcanismDinosaurs diversify
CAMP Eruptions 201.6 Ma Central Atlantic opens. Massive LIP eruptions trigger CO₂ spike and ocean acidification. Marks Triassic-Jurassic boundary.

Jurassic

201 – 145 Ma

▼

Dinosaurs dominate all terrestrial niches. First bird (Archaeopteryx ~150 Ma). Sauropods reach enormous sizes. Pangaea continues rifting.

Sauropod Giants

175 – 145 Ma

▼

Brachiosaurus, Diplodocus, Stegosaurus. Morrison Formation fauna. First bird Archaeopteryx (~150 Ma). Mammals diversify into tree-climbers.

First birdsSauropod peak
Archaeopteryx ~150 Ma Transitional feathered dinosaur-bird. Had claws, teeth, and long bony tail — but also feathered wings and could fly.
Diplodocus ~155 Ma 27 m long sauropod. Whip-like tail could crack supersonic. Browsed cycads and conifers in semi-arid floodplains.

Cretaceous

145 – 66 Ma

▼

Angiosperms (flowering plants) radiate. T. rex and Triceratops. High sea levels. Ends with K-Pg asteroid impact killing non-avian dinosaurs.

Early Cretaceous

145 – 100 Ma

▼

First flowering plants (~130 Ma). Spinosaurus swims. Feathered dinosaurs diversify (Microraptor, Yi). Southern continents separate.

Flowering plantsFeathered dinosaursGondwana splits

Microraptor ~125 Ma Four-winged feathered dinosaur from Liaoning. Evidence for iridescent plumage. Glided between trees.

Late Cretaceous Giants

100 – 66 Ma

▼

T. rex, Triceratops, Hadrosaurs. Seas cover 1/3 of continents. Mosasaurs dominate oceans. Deccan Traps volcanism begins.

T. rex evolvesDeccan Traps
Tyrannosaurus rex 68–66 Ma Apex predator — 12 m, 8 tonnes, 60 cm teeth. Forward-facing eyes for binocular vision. Possibly feathered as juveniles.

K-Pg Extinction

66 Ma

▼

Chicxulub asteroid (10 km). ~76% species extinct. All non-avian dinosaurs gone. Triggers mammal radiation. Deccan Traps compound effects.

Mass extinction #5Chicxulub impactMammals radiate
Chicxulub Impact 66.043 Ma Asteroid strikes Yucatán at 20 km/s. Releases energy = 10 billion Hiroshima bombs. Global firestorm, impact winter, acid rain.
Impact Winter 66.0–65.5 Ma Soot and sulfate aerosols block sunlight for years. Global temperatures drop ~10–15°C. Photosynthesis collapses.
Mammal Explosion 65–55 Ma With dinosaurs gone, mammals rapidly diversify into all ecological niches. Within 10 Ma, most modern mammal orders appear.
Cenozoic 66 Ma – Present ▼

Age of Mammals. Grasses & grasslands. Antarctic ice sheet. Primates, hominins, Homo sapiens. Ice Ages. Holocene — our current epoch.

Paleogene

66 – 23 Ma

▼

Mammal diversification. PETM warming event (~56 Ma). India–Asia collision builds Himalayas. Whales return to sea. Eocene–Oligocene cooling.

Paleocene

66 – 56 Ma

▼

Rapid mammal diversification. First primates (~65 Ma). First large mammals. Climate warms. Condylarths give rise to ungulates.

First primatesMammal explosion
Purgatorius ~66 Ma Oldest known primate relative. Small, tree-dwelling insectivore. Survived K-Pg in tree canopy or underground.
PETM 56 Ma Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum: +5–8°C over 20,000 years. Massive carbon release. Driven by volcanism (North Atlantic Igneous Province).

Eocene

56 – 34 Ma

▼

Warmest Cenozoic climate. Protocetids (whale ancestors) enter sea (~50 Ma). Horses (Eohippus) evolve. Perissodactyls and artiodactyls diversify. Antarctica cools.

Whales evolveHorses evolveIndia-Asia collision
Ambulocetus ~49 Ma Semi-aquatic whale ancestor — could walk on land and swim. Transition between land-dwelling Pakicetus and fully aquatic whales.
Eocene–Oligocene Extinction ~34 Ma Drake Passage opens, circumpolar current established, Antarctica glaciates. Significant marine turnover (Grande Coupure in Europe).

Oligocene

34 – 23 Ma

▼

Global cooling. Grasslands begin expanding. Entelodonts (hell pigs), rhinoceroses, primitive cats. Antarctic permanent ice cap forms.

Antarctic ice sheetGrasslands spread
Antarctic Glaciation ~34 Ma East Antarctic Ice Sheet forms permanently. Seals and penguins colonize Antarctic shores. Global sea level drops 60–70 m.

Neogene

23 – 2.6 Ma

▼

Miocene

23 – 5.3 Ma

▼

Grassland expansion. Horse, elephant, bovid radiation. First hominoids (~20 Ma). Proconsul. Megalodon (largest shark) active. Kelp forests evolve.

Pliocene

5.3 – 2.6 Ma

▼

Australopithecus in Africa (~3.9 Ma). Panama Isthmus closes (3.0 Ma). Climate cools. First stone tools (~3.3 Ma)? Northern hemisphere glaciation begins.

Quaternary

2.6 Ma – Present

▼

Ice Ages cycle every 100,000 years. Homo sapiens evolve (~300 Ka). Megafauna extinctions. Agriculture. Industrial age. Anthropocene.

Pleistocene

2.6 Ma – 11,700 years ago

▼

50+ glacial cycles. Woolly mammoths, sabertooth cats. Homo sapiens (~300 Ka), migration out of Africa (~70 Ka), megafauna extinctions.

Homo sapiensMegafauna extinctionIce Ages
Homo erectus ~1.9 Ma First hominin to leave Africa. Used fire and Acheulean tools. Reached Java and China. Brain ~900 cc.
Homo sapiens ~300 Ka Earliest modern humans in Jebel Irhoud, Morocco. Anatomically modern skull shape. Brain ~1,400 cc.
Out of Africa ~70 Ka Homo sapiens leave Africa during glacial period (low sea levels). Interbreed with Neanderthals and Denisovans. Spread globally by ~15 Ka.
Pleistocene Megafauna Ext. 50–10 Ka Mammoths, mastodons, giant sloths, cave lions go extinct. Climate change + human hunting are leading explanations.

Holocene

11,700 ya – Present

▼

Post-ice-age warm period. Agriculture (~10,000 BC). Civilizations. Industrial Revolution. Human-dominated biosphere. 6th mass extinction underway.

AgricultureCivilizations6th mass extinctionAnthropocene
Neolithic Revolution ~10,000 BCE Independent emergence of agriculture in Fertile Crescent, China, Americas. Sedentary civilizations, population growth, deforestation begin.
Holocene Extinction 10,000 BCE – now Current extinction rate 100–1,000× background rate. ~1M species threatened. Habitat loss, climate change, overexploitation.
Anthropocene ~1950 CE Proposed epoch: humans as dominant geological force. CO₂ at 420+ ppm (highest in 3+ million years). Plastics, nuclear fallout as geological markers.
